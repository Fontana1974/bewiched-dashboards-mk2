The Roasters' Club

A tiered rewards system for the Bewiched app, designed to do one thing the current scheme doesn't: protect margin while making the customer feel more rewarded, not less. Today we hand back cash. Cash is the single most expensive thing we can give away — every £1 off is £1 of gross profit gone. This concept swaps cash-off for rewards that cost us pennies but feel like pounds, wrapped in an earned-status ladder that drives frequency, ATV and retention.

Ladders to the VTO — 1-yr targets: +10% lfl, 18% profit, app share climbing. 5-yr: £20m / 40 stores. This protects the profit line and the lfl line at the same time, using the brand's own Unique — Freshly Roasted Coffee — as the reward currency.

The problem in one line

Cash-off rewards — £1 / £3 / £5 Off — dominate the redemption mix, and they are the most margin-destructive reward we can possibly give. A £1-off costs us a full £1 of gross profit. A free latte feels like £3.40 to the customer but costs us roughly 42p in milk, beans and cup. We are currently giving away the expensive thing and under-using the cheap-but-valuable one. That's backwards.

The core move: stop giving cash, start giving coffee

Every reward sits somewhere on the gap between what it costs us and what it feels worth to the customer. The whole strategy is to push redemptions into the top-right of that gap — high perceived value, near-zero cost to us — and tier the access so it drives behaviour.

The ladder — four earned roasts

Status is earned on a rolling 90-day window of spend, so it has to be maintained — that's what drives repeat frequency. Thresholds are anchored to the real spend distribution of our 20,762 active app customers (see the data below), set so each rung feels reachable but the top stays rare. Naming uses the roast journey — our own Freshly Roasted Coffee Unique — so the ladder is the brand story.

Why rolling, not lifetime. Lifetime tiers reward yesterday's spend forever and cost us margin on autopilot. A rolling 90-day window means status must be maintained — that's the lever that lifts visit frequency, which is the real prize given app ATV is already +19%.

The points trick. Beans (points) are deliberately generous-feeling — 5 per £1 means a £3 coffee earns 15 Beans, and a free coffee lands at ~150 Beans. The customer feels showered in points; we're only ever redeeming them against an item that costs us pennies. Big numbers, small cost.

Margin: before vs after

Same volume of reward "value" handed to customers (~£34.4k QTD), modelled under today's cash-heavy mix vs the concept's coffee-led mix. The customer feeling stays the same or improves; the real cost to us roughly halves and then some.

Today — cash-led mix

~75% of reward value is cash-off (~£25.8k, lost pound-for-pound) + ~25% free drinks (~£1.1k real cost). Real GP cost ≈ £26.9k QTD for £34.4k of perceived value — 78p in the pound.

Concept — coffee-led mix

Flip to ~20% cash-off (capped) + ~80% high-margin items. Real GP cost ≈ £10.9k QTD for the same £34.4k of perceived value — 32p in the pound. Indicative protection ~£16k/quarter (~£64k/yr), before breakage and frequency upside.

The behavioural engine ("illusion of value")

The levers that make a low-cost scheme feel generous, and quietly work in our favour.

💎

Generous-feeling points

5 Beans per £1 and big redemption numbers make the scheme feel rich — while every redemption lands on an item costing us pennies.

⏳

Breakage

Beans expire after 6 months of inactivity; tier status decays on the rolling window. Industry breakage runs 10–25% of issued value — reward never claimed is margin retained.

🎯

Aspirational thresholds

Reserve is visible to all but reached by ~3–4%. Most aspire and lift their spend chasing it; few fully exploit the perks. The gap is the win.

🎁

Surprise & delight

Random "Golden Cup" free upgrades and double-Bean weekends — cheap, memorable, and timed to fill trough trading periods.

⚡

Status perks that cost ~£0

Skip-the-queue order-ahead, early access to launches, name on the in-store screen. Zero COGS, high felt status — and they pull customers into store.

🤝

Attach, not discount

Treat-pairings (cookie + coffee) and bring-a-friend rewards generate incremental sales rather than discounting an existing one — reward spend that grows revenue.

What I need you to weigh in on

This is a concept to react to. The structure works on the data, but five calls are genuinely yours to make.

1 · Spend-based or visit-based tiers?
Spend-based (proposed) rewards basket size and protects ATV. Visit-based (P75 = 6, P90 = 13, P95 = 21 visits / 90 days) would drive raw frequency harder — better for the lfl line, but a £2.50 daily-coffee customer climbs as fast as a £6 one.
My lean: spend-based primary, with a visit-count "fast-track" to the next tier as a secondary trigger.
2 · Four tiers, or three?
Four gives a clear aspirational top (Reserve) and more steps to climb. Three is simpler to run and communicate at the counter. The data supports either — P75 / P90 / P95 are clean break points.
My lean: four — the Reserve badge is where the "illusion of value" pays off hardest.
3 · Do we keep cash-off at all?
Keeping a small, capped cash-off (e.g. lapsed-customer win-back only) is a useful blunt instrument. But every pound of it is a pound of GP. The concept caps it at ~20% of reward value and falling.
My lean: keep it only as a win-back lever, never a default earn.
4 · Rolling window length — 90 days?
90 days matches our data window and feels fair. 30 days drives frequency harder but risks demoting good customers after one quiet fortnight (holidays, illness) and feeling punitive.
My lean: 90 days, reviewed monthly so status updates feel live, not cliff-edge.
5 · Does "The Roasters' Club" land?
It leans on Freshly Roasted Coffee — a genuine Unique — and the roast-journey naming is ownable. But it's a customer-facing name and needs to sit alongside, not clash with, BrewCrew internally.
My lean: strong contender, but worth testing 2–3 names with the team before we commit.
Caveat on the numbers. COGS and perceived-value figures are working estimates for the concept and the margin model is illustrative — it holds perceived reward value constant and varies only the mix. Before any launch we'd lock COGS against real recipe costings and model the actual current redemption split (£1 / £3 / £5 / free-drink) rather than the assumed 75/25.
